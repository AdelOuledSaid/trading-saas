01

Multi-Timeframe Top-Down Analysis

Reading the same reality from multiple altitudes simultaneously

Level 2 taught you to build HTF bias on a single instrument. Level 3 teaches you to apply that principle systematically across all timeframes and multiple instruments — building a coherent, hierarchical reading of the entire market before identifying a single setup.

The principle: the weekly chart sets the context. The daily chart reveals the structure. The 4H chart shows the exploitable zone. The 1H chart refines the entry. These four levels work as a coherent system — not as independent charts to browse randomly.

Top-down analysis defined

A structured method of reading markets by starting from the highest timeframe (weekly or monthly) and descending to execution timeframes (4H, 1H), ensuring every lower-timeframe action is interpreted within its larger context.

The 4-level hierarchy

TimeframePrimary functionKey question
WeeklyMacro context & dominant trendWhat is the dominant direction over the past 3–6 months?
DailyStructure & key zonesWhere are the major S/R zones? What is the daily bias?
4HOpportunity identificationWhere is the exploitable setup forming? Is there OB/FVG at a key level?
1H / 15MEntry timing & confirmationWhere exactly is the LTF confirmation? What is the precise entry trigger?
The most common error at Level 3

Starting the analysis on the 15M chart and working upward — rationalizing a trade that "feels right" on the LTF. Top-down means strictly top-first. If the weekly chart shows no clear bias, the 15M setup is not valid regardless of how clean it looks locally.

02

Complete Market Planning Protocol

The professional operator's pre-session routine

A professional operator does not sit down at the screen and start looking for trades. They sit down and execute a preparation protocol that organizes the market, ranks opportunities, and defines the conditions under which they will act — before the first candle of the session opens.

The Level 3 market planning protocol — 7 steps

  • Step 1 — Global macro check (5 min): Check DXY, US100, VIX direction on daily. What is the risk-on/off environment?
  • Step 2 — BTC weekly and daily context (3 min): What is BTC's dominant structure? Where is BTC.D trending?
  • Step 3 — Asset ranking (5 min): Which 3 instruments show the cleanest structure and highest confluence today?
  • Step 4 — Zone identification (5 min): Mark the top 2 zones per ranked asset on the 4H chart.
  • Step 5 — Scenario tree (5 min): For each zone, define Scenario A (primary), Scenario B (alternate), Scenario C (invalidation).
  • Step 6 — Exposure check (2 min): How many active positions do I have? What is total exposure? What macro theme do they express?
  • Step 7 — Session triggers (2 min): At what exact price or candle will I act? Set price alerts — close the charts.
The discipline of preparation

A trader who runs this 27-minute protocol before every session will make fewer impulsive decisions, identify better opportunities, and avoid the single biggest source of retail losses: reactive trading driven by screen time rather than preparation.

03

Scenario A / B / C Construction

From single conviction to structured probabilistic planning

Level 2 taught you to define one scenario and its invalidation. Level 3 teaches you to hold three simultaneously — not as a way to justify any outcome, but as a framework that prevents fixation bias (the single most expensive cognitive error in trading).

Scenario A — Primary (60–70% probability)

  • Most aligned with all timeframes
  • Supported by macro environment
  • Full confidence size: 1.5–2% risk
  • Specific trigger and invalidation defined

Scenario B — Alternate (20–30% probability)

  • Different direction or timing
  • Triggered by specific structural event
  • Reduced size: 0.5–1% risk
  • Requires Scenario A invalidation first

Scenario C — Invalidation (remaining probability)

  • The condition that makes both A and B wrong
  • Forces full exit of existing positions
  • Must be a precise price level — not a feeling
  • Triggers complete reassessment

How to switch scenarios

  • Scenario A active: monitor for trigger or invalidation
  • A invalidated: automatically shift attention to B
  • B triggered: enter with reduced conviction size
  • C triggered: exit all — no new positions for 24h
Applied example — BTC at $60,000 support

Scenario A: BTC holds $59,800 zone, forms 4H bullish OB reaction, sweeps equal lows at $59,700, 1H CHoCH confirms → long to $65,000. Scenario B: BTC loses $59,800, retests $57,500 weekly structure, 4H bearish structure remains intact → short. Scenario C: BTC drops below $55,000 on high volume → full structural breakdown, no position.

Inter-Market Correlations — Applied

How DXY, US100, BTC.D and Gold connect your trades

Level 2 introduced the concept of HTF bias on a single instrument. Level 3 introduces the inter-market layer: how macroeconomic assets interact, how they confirm or contradict each other, and how reading multiple markets simultaneously gives you a composite view that single-instrument analysis cannot provide.

The 4 core macro relationships every operator must read daily

RelationshipDirectionWhat it tells you
DXY ↑ + BTC ↓Negative correlationDollar strength = headwind for risk assets. Reduce long crypto exposure.
US100 ↑ + BTC ↑Positive correlationRisk-on environment — crypto longs have macro tailwind.
BTC.D ↑BTC outperforming altsCapital concentrated in BTC — altcoin longs carry higher risk.
BTC.D ↓Alts outperforming BTCRotation phase — select altcoin opportunities may outperform.
Gold ↑ + US100 ↓Risk-off signalStress or uncertainty — reduce all risk asset exposure.
The macro composite reading

Before entering any trade, ask: what is the macro composite telling me right now? If DXY is rising, US100 is falling and Gold is rising — that is a clear risk-off signal. Trading a BTC long in that environment requires exceptional structural conviction and reduced size.

Level 3 rule

A setup that has structural confluence (Level 2) AND macro tailwind (Level 3) is worth at least 1.5x the conviction of a setup with structure alone. Macro alignment does not guarantee success — it shifts probabilities in your favor.

05

Asset Selection by Relative Clarity

Allocating attention toward the most readable instruments

Professional traders do not watch 50 instruments equally. They develop a method for ranking instruments by "readability" — the clarity of the current structure, the quality of available levels, and the alignment of all timeframes.

An instrument where the weekly, daily and 4H all agree on direction, where a clear OB or FVG exists, and where the macro environment is aligned, is worth 5x the analytical attention of an instrument where structure is mixed and macro is contradicting the local setup.

Asset selection matrix — score each instrument

CriterionScoreQuestion
Weekly structure clarity0–3Is the weekly trend unambiguous?
Daily zone quality0–3Is there a clean OB or S/R zone on the daily?
4H confluence score0–2Does the 4H show OB + FVG + liquidity sweep opportunity?
Macro alignment0–2Does the trade direction align with DXY/US100/BTC.D environment?

Total possible: 10 points. Focus exclusively on instruments scoring 7+. Instruments below 5 are removed from the watchlist for that session.

Attention as a scarce resource

Your analytical attention is limited. Spreading it across 20 instruments equally guarantees you will miss the best opportunities while wasting time on mediocre ones. The professional's edge is not more watchlist items — it is better filtering of which items deserve attention.

06

Global Exposure Management

Managing risk across a portfolio of simultaneous ideas

At Level 2, you managed one trade at a time. At Level 3, you often have multiple ideas active simultaneously. This creates portfolio-level risk that is invisible at the individual trade level — and requires explicit management.

Hidden concentration risk at Level 3

If you are long BTC, long ETH, long SOL and long US100 simultaneously, you likely have 4 expressions of a single macro bet: risk-on. A single macro shift — dollar spike, Fed hawkishness, geopolitical stress — can hit all 4 positions simultaneously, multiplying your effective loss beyond any individual position sizing.

Global exposure framework for Level 3 operators

  • Maximum concurrent risk: No more than 5% total account risk across all open positions simultaneously
  • Theme concentration limit: No more than 3% total risk on positions expressing the same macro theme
  • Correlation check: Before each new entry, check how it correlates with existing positions
  • Asymmetry principle: Allocate more size to A+ setups, less to B setups — never equal across all ideas
Portfolio check — practical example

Active positions: Long BTC (1.5% risk), Long ETH (1% risk), Long US100 (1% risk). Total risk: 3.5%. Macro theme: risk-on (all three). Theme concentration: 3.5% on risk-on theme — approaching the 3% limit. Next trade: if bullish SOL setup appears, maximum 0.5% risk. Or wait for one of the existing positions to close first before adding more risk-on exposure.

Advanced Pre-Session Routine

The 30-minute protocol that separates operators from reactive traders

A reactive trader opens their platform, sees price moving, and tries to decide in real time. An operator has already decided what they will do before the market moves — and simply executes the plan that was written in a calm, analytical state.

The Level 3 daily routine (30 minutes total)

Pre-market (15 min)

  • Check overnight macro moves (DXY, futures, BTC overnight range)
  • Update weekly/daily bias on top 3 instruments
  • Identify active scenarios and check if A/B/C status changed
  • Mark new zones that formed overnight
  • Check economic calendar for the session
  • Calculate current total exposure
  • Define maximum new exposure for the day

Market hours (ongoing)

  • Execute only — no new analysis during live market
  • Act only on pre-defined triggers (price alerts)
  • No screen-watching between trigger checks
  • Scenario switch only on defined criteria — not on feel

Post-session (15 min)

  • Record each trade with setup grade
  • Note whether macro context was correctly read
  • Update scenario status for next session
  • Calculate session P&L in R-multiples (not dollars)
  • Write one transferable lesson

Weekly review (30 min, Sunday)

  • Update weekly bias on all watchlist instruments
  • Review macro changes from the prior week
  • Calculate weekly expectancy and setup grade performance
  • Identify the top 3 recurring patterns this week

Sector & Narrative Rotation in Crypto

Reading capital flow between sectors to anticipate the next leader

In traditional finance, sector rotation describes capital moving between defensive and growth sectors based on the economic cycle. Crypto has an analogous dynamic: capital rotates between BTC → large caps → mid caps → small caps → speculative tokens — and understanding where in this rotation cycle the market is determines which instruments to prioritize.

Crypto rotation sequence

  • Phase 1 — BTC accumulation: BTC dominance rising, alts flat or declining. Focus: BTC only.
  • Phase 2 — ETH rotation: BTC consolidates, ETH outperforms. ETH/BTC pair rising. Add ETH longs.
  • Phase 3 — Large cap rotation: SOL, BNB, AVAX outperform. BTC.D declining further. Selective large-cap longs.
  • Phase 4 — Mid/small cap distribution: High beta altcoins peak, smart money rotating back to BTC. Warning: late-cycle risk.
Practical reading — BTC.D chart

The BTC Dominance chart (BTC.D on TradingView) tells you instantly where in the rotation cycle the market is. BTC.D above 55% and rising: Phase 1, BTC only. BTC.D 50–55% and declining: Phase 2–3, ETH and large caps. BTC.D below 45%: Phase 4, late cycle — extreme caution.

Risk Adaptation to Macro Conditions

Dynamically adjusting exposure based on the environment

Level 1 taught fixed risk per trade (1–2%). Level 3 introduces dynamic risk adjustment — systematically changing your standard risk size based on the quality of the macro environment.

Macro environmentCharacteristicsRisk adjustment
Clear risk-onUS100 trending, DXY weak, BTC.D stableStandard or slightly above standard (1–2%)
Mixed signalsConflicting macro indicators, unclear regimeReduce by 30–50% (0.5–1%)
Risk-offVIX rising, DXY surging, US100 breaking downReduce by 60–75% or no new trades
High uncertainty eventFed meeting, CPI, major geopolitical eventNo new positions 2h before and after
Personal drawdown > 5%Personal performance threshold breachedHalve all position sizes until recovered
The environment multiplier

Position size = (Base risk %) × Environment multiplier. In a clear risk-on environment, multiplier = 1.0. In mixed signals, multiplier = 0.5. In risk-off, multiplier = 0.25. This ensures your largest positions are always in your highest-probability environments — not in noise.

Performance Measurement — R-Multiples System

Why measuring in dollars is misleading and what to use instead

Most traders measure performance in dollars or percentages. This is misleading because it conflates decision quality with position size. A $500 win on a $50,000 account is not the same quality as a $500 win on a $5,000 account.

R-multiples solve this: 1R always means "I risked this amount and got back exactly that amount." A +2R trade means the profit was twice the risk. A –0.5R trade means you lost half your planned risk (partial exit or tight stop).

Key R-multiple performance metrics

MetricCalculationHealthy range
Average R per tradeSum of all R outcomes ÷ number of trades+0.3R to +0.8R per trade
System expectancy(Win rate × avg win R) − (loss rate × avg loss R)Positive — any value above 0
Best setup grade RAverage R for A+ setups onlyShould be 2–3x higher than B setups
Max consecutive lossLongest losing streak in R termsShould trigger drawdown protocol if > –5R total
The compound insight

When you measure in R, you will see clearly that A+ setups produce +0.9R average while B setups produce +0.1R average. This single data point — derived from your own trading history — proves more convincingly than any theory that you should trade exclusively A+ setups.

Session Preparation — Putting It All Together

A single complete example of Level 3 planning in action

This module presents a complete, end-to-end example of how a Level 3 operator prepares a trading session — integrating every concept from Modules 1–10 into a single coherent workflow.

Example session preparation — Tuesday, crypto market

  • Macro check: DXY at 104.5, testing resistance. US100 in minor pullback from ATH. BTC.D at 52%, declining. Risk environment: cautious risk-on.
  • BTC weekly/daily bias: Weekly HH/HL intact, daily showing consolidation between $59,000 and $62,000. No clear BOS yet. Bias: neutral-to-bullish pending daily break.
  • Asset ranking: ETH scores 8/10 (weekly uptrend clean, daily OB at $3,200, BTC.D declining). SOL scores 7/10. BTC scores 5/10 (in range, unclear). ETH and SOL prioritized.
  • Scenarios for ETH: A: ETH reacts at $3,200 daily OB, sweeps $3,150 liquidity, 4H bullish CHoCH → long to $3,450. B: ETH loses $3,200, tests $2,950 weekly structure → reassess. C: ETH daily close below $2,900 → bear scenario, no longs.
  • Exposure check: No active positions. Total risk available: 5%. Allocate max 2% for ETH entry if Scenario A triggers, 1.5% for SOL if opportunity appears.
  • Triggers set: Price alert at $3,220 (ETH approaching OB). Alert at $3,145 (potential sweep level). Charts closed — alerts will notify.
The professional mindset shift

A Level 3 operator does not "watch the market." They prepare a decision tree in advance, set the triggers that activate each branch, and then step away. The trade executes on the trigger, not on screen-induced emotion. This is the transition from reactive trader to systematic operator.

From Level 3 to Level 4 — The Institutional Gap

Understanding what separates the operator from the institutional desk

Level 3 makes you a systematic, structured operator. You plan before acting, you manage global exposure, you read inter-market relationships. This is already the top 5% of retail trading capability.

Level 4 introduces the institutional layer that goes beyond structured individual trading: macro regime identification as a primary filter, managing a portfolio of 6–10 simultaneous ideas across multiple instruments, scaling positions in and out with institutional precision, running a professional review framework, and building a repeatable desk routine that operates at a consistent quality standard regardless of recent performance.

Level 3 operator

  • Plans sessions systematically
  • Manages 2–3 concurrent positions
  • Reads macro environment as context
  • Has scenario A/B/C per trade
  • Measures in R-multiples
  • Graded journal

Level 4 institutional desk

  • Regime mapping as primary filter
  • Portfolio of 6–10 ideas simultaneously
  • Macro inter-market drives asset selection
  • Scenario tree across the portfolio
  • Scaling models (scout/core/runner)
  • 6-dimension professional review
The transition principle

Level 4 is not a harder version of Level 3. It is a different way of thinking — from individual trades to a system of ideas. Mastering Level 3 completely (90+ sessions of disciplined execution) is the prerequisite for Level 4 to be meaningful rather than overwhelming.
