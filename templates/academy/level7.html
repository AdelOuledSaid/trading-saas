The Prop Firm Landscape

Understanding the professional evaluation ecosystem

Proprietary trading firms (prop firms) provide capital to traders who demonstrate consistent profitability through a structured evaluation. Passing a prop firm challenge is the most accessible path to managing significant capital without personal risk — but it requires understanding the rules precisely, because many are designed to fail undisciplined traders.

Firm typeModelEvaluationTypical split
Two-phase evaluationPhase 1: profit target. Phase 2: consistency. Funded if both passed.8–10% profit target, 5% daily loss, 10% max drawdown70–85% to trader
One-phase challengeSingle phase with higher profit target10% profit target, 5% daily loss75–80% to trader
Instant fundingNo evaluation — smaller initial capitalOngoing profit target and loss limits50–70% to trader
Revenue shareNo evaluation fee — lower profit splitMonthly consistency requirements50–60% to trader
Prop firm selection criteria

Before paying any evaluation fee, verify: regulated or reputable history (check community reviews), clear payout history, realistic drawdown rules, no hidden "scaling plan" that limits payouts, and transparent rule documentation. Many prop firms have failed to pay traders — due diligence is not optional.

02

Funded Account Risk Management

Operating under hard constraints — the professional discipline test

Managing a funded account is the ultimate test of every discipline principle in Levels 1–6. The rules are strict, the consequences of violation are immediate (account closure), and the psychological pressure of trading someone else's capital is significant.

The 3 critical rules of funded account management

  • Daily loss limit (typically 4–5%): If your account loses this % in one day, it closes automatically. Strategy: never risk more than 1% per trade. After 2 consecutive losses in a day, stop trading for that session — you are 2 trades from account closure.
  • Maximum total drawdown (typically 8–10%): If cumulative losses reach this from the starting balance, account closes. Strategy: treat 6% drawdown as your personal red alert. Do not wait for the firm's limit — your drawdown protocol must trigger first.
  • Consistency rule (some firms): No single day's profit can exceed a defined % of total profit. Strategy: if you have an exceptional day, scale down for the rest of the evaluation period to avoid violating consistency requirements.
The funded account mindset shift

Your personal trading uses 1–2% risk per trade. On a funded account, you may need to reduce to 0.5% per trade to ensure the daily loss limit is not breached even by 3 consecutive losses. Funded account success rewards capital preservation over profit maximization — the opposite of what most traders instinctively pursue.

Building a Verified Track Record

The foundation of professional credibility

A track record is a verified, auditable history of trading performance. It is the single most important asset for any trader seeking external capital — without it, you are asking investors to trust claims, not evidence.

Track record requirements for institutional credibility

  • Minimum duration: 12 months of live trading. 6 months is insufficient — it cannot span multiple market regimes.
  • Verification: Brokerage statements, not screenshots. Myfxbook or third-party audit service for Forex. On-chain proof for crypto.
  • Risk-adjusted metrics: Absolute return is meaningless without context. A 40% return achieved through 50% drawdown is not investable. Sharpe ratio and max drawdown are the minimum required metrics.
  • Consistency evidence: Monthly breakdown showing no single "lucky month" distorts the average. Investors need to see consistency, not outliers.
Myfxbook verified track record

For Forex accounts, Myfxbook.com provides free, broker-verified performance tracking that is widely accepted by prop firms and private investors. Connect your broker account, enable "Gain Chart" public sharing. For crypto, use a portfolio tracker with API connection (CoinStats, Koinly) combined with brokerage statement exports.

Investor Reporting — Monthly & Quarterly

Communicating performance clearly and professionally

Professional traders who manage external capital produce formal monthly reports. These documents serve two functions: they maintain investor trust through transparency, and they force the trader to analyze performance rigorously and explain it clearly.

Monthly report structure

  • Executive summary (1 paragraph): Performance this month, key market conditions, strategy behavior, outlook.
  • Performance metrics: Monthly return %, drawdown from high-water mark, Sharpe ratio, trades taken, win rate.
  • Equity curve chart: Cumulative performance since inception — monthly granularity.
  • Attribution analysis: Which markets, strategies or regimes drove performance? What detracted?
  • Risk metrics: Max daily loss, current drawdown, exposure by asset class.
  • Outlook: Current market regime assessment, positioning rationale for next month.
  • Verification: Brokerage statement or third-party verification link.
Transparency builds trust

Never hide a bad month in a report. Explain poor performance clearly: what caused it, whether it was strategy-driven or random variance, and what specific protocol changes (if any) are being implemented. Investors who discover hidden problems destroy relationships. Investors who see transparent acknowledgment of difficulties and clear responses often become long-term partners.

Capital Raising — First Principles

How professional traders access external capital

Capital raising is not about selling — it is about demonstrating evidence and removing risk for the capital provider. The track record from Module 3 is the foundation. The process is structured to build trust incrementally.

The 4-stage capital raising progression

  • Stage 1 — Prop firm capital: Cheapest credibility test. Pass a prop firm evaluation to prove you can manage disciplined risk under rules. Scale to the maximum allocation available ($100K–$1M).
  • Stage 2 — Friends & family: First external capital with low formal requirements. Structure as a performance fee arrangement with full transparency. Minimum 12-month track record required.
  • Stage 3 — Accredited investors: Formal agreements required (consult a lawyer). Minimum 24-month track record, formal reporting, clear fee structure, risk disclosure documentation.
  • Stage 4 — Institutional capital: Fund structure required (SMA or LP). 3+ year track record minimum. External auditor. Regulatory registration depending on jurisdiction and AUM.
Legal warning

Managing capital for others without proper regulatory registration is illegal in most jurisdictions above certain thresholds. Before accepting any external capital, consult a financial lawyer in your jurisdiction. The rules differ significantly between countries — this module provides education, not legal advice.

Safe harbor — the 15-client rule (US)

In the US, investment advisers with fewer than 15 clients and under $25M AUM may qualify for an exemption from SEC registration under the Investment Advisers Act. However, state registration may still be required. This is an educational overview only — verify current thresholds with a qualified securities attorney.

Minimum compliance checklist for any external capital

  • Written investment management agreement signed by all parties
  • Clear fee structure disclosed in writing before capital is received
  • Risk disclosure document detailing all material risks
  • Separate trading account — never commingled with personal funds
  • Monthly statements provided to all capital providers
  • Legal counsel confirming structure is compliant in your jurisdiction

AML & KYC for Professional Traders

Anti-money laundering obligations and know-your-customer procedures

Any professional trader managing third-party capital must understand Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and Know-Your-Customer (KYC) obligations. Even small operations face legal exposure if they accept funds from unverified sources.

KYC requirements for capital acceptance

  • Identity verification: Government-issued ID (passport or national ID) for all capital providers
  • Source of funds: Documentation showing the legitimate origin of invested capital
  • Address verification: Recent utility bill or bank statement confirming residential address
  • Politically Exposed Persons (PEP) check: Mandatory screening for government officials or their close associates
  • Record retention: All KYC documents must be retained for minimum 5–7 years depending on jurisdiction
AML red flags — when to refuse capital

Refuse any capital from: sources that cannot explain the origin of funds; cash or cryptocurrency from anonymous wallets with no verifiable history; investors who request unusual transaction structures; individuals on international sanctions lists (OFAC, UN, EU). Accepting funds from these sources exposes you to serious criminal liability regardless of intent.

Fund Structure Types

SMA, LP, LLC — choosing the right legal vehicle

As a professional trader scales beyond a few informal capital providers, a formal legal structure becomes necessary for investor protection, tax efficiency and operational clarity.

Start with SMA

The Separately Managed Account model requires the least legal complexity — the investor maintains their own brokerage account and grants you a limited power of attorney to trade it. This means no fund registration, lower legal costs, and full transparency for the investor. It is the recommended structure for first external capital up to $1–2M.

Fee Structures — 2/20 and Alternatives

How professional traders get paid for performance

The fee structure defines how a trader is compensated for managing external capital. It must be disclosed clearly, agreed in writing before capital is received, and structured to align the trader's incentives with the investor's interests.

StructureDescriptionBest for
Management fee only1–2% annual fee on AUM, charged monthlyLower-risk, consistent strategies
Performance fee only20–30% of profits above high-water markAligns incentives — pay only for results
2/202% management + 20% performance above HWMInstitutional standard for hedge funds
1/10 (accessible version)1% management + 10% performanceEarly-stage operators attracting first capital
Profit split only30–50% of net profits — no management feeProp firm model, friends & family
High-water mark

A high-water mark ensures performance fees are only charged on new all-time highs of the investor's account value. If the account drops 10% and then recovers 10%, no performance fee is owed — the trader must first make back the losses before earning performance fees on new profits. This critical investor protection should be in every fee agreement.

Scaling a Trading Operation

The operational challenges that emerge at $1M, $10M and $100M AUM

Most trading strategies work differently at scale. Strategies that produce excellent results at $50K capital face execution challenges, slippage issues and structural limitations at $5M. Understanding these scaling challenges before they occur prevents the performance degradation that destroys track records at the exact moment external capital arrives.

The 3 scaling challenges

  • Market impact: At $100K, your orders don't move markets. At $10M in a small-cap crypto, your entry itself creates slippage. Scale requires either moving to higher-liquidity instruments, longer timeframes, or splitting entries across time.
  • Strategy capacity: Every edge has a maximum capital capacity. A scalping strategy may generate +2% monthly on $50K but only +0.3% on $5M because the entry zones can't absorb the full position. Know your strategy's capacity limit before marketing to investors.
  • Operational complexity: Managing one account requires 1 person. Managing 20 SMAs requires systems, reporting, compliance and potentially additional staff. Build systems before they are needed — not after.
Strategy capacity test

To estimate your strategy's capacity, track slippage on entries as you increase position size. If you consistently experience more than 0.3% slippage at a given size, you have reached the liquidity ceiling for that setup type. The maximum capital for that strategy is approximately the size at which slippage starts degrading your edge.

The Professional Trader's Business Infrastructure

Systems, technology and operations for a sustainable trading business

A professional trading business requires infrastructure beyond a good strategy and a brokerage account. This module covers the operational systems that allow a trader to scale, delegate and maintain performance quality as complexity increases.

Technology stack

  • TradingView Pro+ (multi-device, alerts)
  • Python environment (Level 6 toolkit)
  • CRM for investor communication
  • Excel/Google Sheets for performance tracking
  • Myfxbook or third-party verification
  • Secure password manager for account access

Operational documents

  • Investment Management Agreement (per investor)
  • Risk Disclosure Document
  • Monthly/quarterly report template
  • KYC file per investor
  • Trading journal (personal system document)
  • Drawdown response protocol (written)

Daily operational routine

  • Pre-market: planning protocol (Level 3)
  • Session: execution only (Level 4)
  • Post-session: journal + performance update
  • Weekly: investor email / performance note
  • Monthly: formal report sent to all investors
  • Quarterly: strategy audit (Level 5)

Risk governance

  • Hard daily loss limit per account
  • Weekly max loss limit across all accounts
  • Monthly drawdown threshold triggers review
  • Independent drawdown protocol enforced
  • No co-mingling of personal and client funds
  • External backup for all trading accounts
